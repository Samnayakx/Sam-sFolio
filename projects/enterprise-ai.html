Overview

How might 3D product visualization improve fit confidence and reduce apparel returns?

Gap Inc. integrated Drapr's 3D fit technology across brands to reduce ecommerce return rates driven by poor fit. I adapted the tool for Old Navy denim, transforming it from a shirt-fit viewer into a denim-focused experience addressing thigh, calf, and inseam complexity.

Problem

Online apparel returns are costing brands billions, and poor fit is the primary driver.

Online returns are a major unsolved problem in fashion retail. For denim specifically, shoppers struggle with inconsistent sizing across styles, an inability to visualize fit across body zones, and a lack of tools that address denim-specific fit complexity.

The existing Drapr viewer was built for shirts, creating a mismatch in fit complexity and user expectations for denim.

Research

50+ interviews revealed that denim fit is uniquely unpredictable, and users know it.

I conducted 50+ user interviews with male shoppers aged 25-45. No prior denim fit tool existed at Old Navy, so the goal was to understand raw frustrations, unspoken expectations, and the gap between what users needed and what was available.

Users reported inconsistent sizing across denim styles, guessed measurements from past purchases, and evaluated fit by body zone rather than by generic size labels.

Design Solution

A 4-step personalization flow centered on visual fit confidence.

The information architecture was redesigned around clarity, personalization, and ease of decision-making. Users wanted visual confidence, low friction, and the ability to compare sizes dynamically, not another technical questionnaire.

The final flow moves through height, waist, avatar adjustment, and 3D fit preview, with controls tuned to the body zones that matter for denim.

From V1 feedback to V2 improvements

V1 made size comparison easier but lacked denim-specific inputs and explanation. V2 added clearer fit descriptors, an explanation layer for recommendations, optional exact measurement input, and explicit controls for thigh, calf, and inseam.

Results

Post-launch metrics confirmed that visual fit confidence drives engagement and purchase behavior.

Measured through user testing, A/B experiments, and usability studies, the project tracked user confidence, engagement depth, and downstream purchase behavior.

The fit viewer reached 120M interactions, increased page visits by 20.3%, and improved click-through on the fit viewer call-to-action by 80%.

Reflection

What I learned

This project reframed fit from a measurement problem to a visualization and trust problem. Users did not need more data; they needed contextual fit information, body-zone control, and transparent explanations connecting recommendations to their actual body.

Fit visualization must be contextual. Thigh room, inseam length, and calf taper are more meaningful than generic size labels.
