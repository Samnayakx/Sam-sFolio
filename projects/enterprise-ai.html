Overview

How might 3D product visualization improve fit confidence and reduce apparel returns?

Gap Inc. integrated Drapr's 3D fit technology across brands to reduce ecommerce return rates driven by poor fit. I adapted the tool for Old Navy denim — transforming it from a shirt-fit viewer into a denim-focused experience addressing thigh, calf, and inseam complexity.

120M Total interactions with the fit viewer
+20.3% Increase in visits post-launch
+80% CTR improvement on fit viewer CTA

Problem

Online apparel returns are costing brands billions — and poor fit is the primary driver.

Online returns are a major unsolved problem in fashion retail. For denim specifically, shoppers struggle with inconsistent sizing across styles, an inability to visualize fit across body zones, and a lack of tools that address denim-specific fit complexity like thigh, calf, and inseam.

$3B Annual loss due to apparel returns
40% Of returns caused by sizing issues

Existing tool limitations

The Drapr viewer was built for shirts — creating a fundamental mismatch in fit complexity and user expectations for denim.

Too many questions

Confusing avatar setup with no guidance left users unsure how to proceed before they saw any value.

Limited inputs

Only chest and height captured — nowhere near sufficient for denim fit complexity across waist, thigh, calf, and inseam.

Too many screens

Multi-step setup created friction before users experienced the product's core value proposition.

Outdated UI

Not aligned with Old Navy's current visual design language, eroding trust before the fit experience began.

Heavy simulation limits

Only one size up or down simulated — built for shirts, unable to handle denim's range of fit variation.

Research

50+ interviews revealed that denim fit is uniquely unpredictable — and users know it.

I conducted 50+ user interviews with male shoppers aged 25–45. No prior denim fit tool existed at Old Navy, so the goal was to understand raw frustrations, unspoken expectations, and the gap between what users needed and what was available.

Design Solution

A 4-step personalization flow centered on visual fit confidence.

The information architecture was redesigned around clarity, personalization, and ease of decision-making. Users wanted visual confidence, low friction, and the ability to compare sizes dynamically — not another technical questionnaire.

1 Height Choose height

Carousel-based selection with live 3D preview updating in real time.

2 Waist Choose waist

Single input matched to denim sizing conventions, not generic measurements.

3 Avatar Adjust avatar

Users refine proportions for thigh, calf, and inseam — the zones that matter for denim.

4 Showcase 3D fit preview

Full 360° denim visualization with size comparison and fit descriptors.

V1 feedback

What worked

Size comparison was helpful. Visualizing fit changes side-by-side improved confidence and reduced hesitation in sizing decisions significantly.

What needed work

Lack of clarity in fit labels. Users were uncertain about recommendation accuracy and needed explanation for "tight" vs "relaxed" — not just a label.

V2 improvements

Size explanation layer

Users clicked naturally out of curiosity — we surfaced a panel explaining why a size was recommended in plain language, not technical terms.

Fit descriptors

Added clear labels for Fitted, Loose, and Relaxed fits — grounded in denim conventions, not generic apparel language that failed in V1.

Measurement UX

Users preferred entering exact measurements when available. Added optional exact-input alongside the simpler carousel to serve both behaviors.

Denim zone controls

Exposed explicit controls for thigh, calf, and inseam — the body zones critical to denim fit that were entirely absent in V1.

Reflection

What I learned

This project reframed fit from a measurement problem to a visualization and trust problem. Users didn't need more data — they needed contextual fit information, body-zone control, and transparent explanations connecting recommendations to their actual body.

Context is everything

Fit visualization must be contextual — thigh room, inseam length, and calf taper are more meaningful than generic size labels.

Explain, don't just recommend

Users need to understand why a size is suggested. Explanation builds trust; a bare recommendation creates doubt and friction.

Control builds trust

Giving users control over body zone inputs — even when they don't use them — increased overall confidence in the recommendation.
